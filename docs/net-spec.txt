00 — THE PROBLEM
Intellectual loneliness


People are more connected than ever, yet intellectually lonelier than ever.


This loneliness is not about lacking relationships or connections — it is about lacking resonance. The feeling of being truly heard by someone who meets your thought where it lives.


Technology has made us highly directed, goal-oriented, optimised. Which quietly erodes the sense of wonder and wandering thought. You hop on a subway and move through physical space with airpods in, eyes on screens.Cities once created accidental proximity around shared context — the unexpected conversation, the encounter that shifts a year. But in this era of answers, the infrastructure for serendipitous encounter has collapsed.


People hunger for genuine encounter but reach for safer substitutes: parasocial relationships, simulated depth. The feeling of being truly heard — what makes us human — is rarer than ever. 


It is not a lack of relationships. It is a lack of resonance.




01 — MISSION
What this is
net. is a platform where shared thoughts become invitations for one-on-one conversations with strangers who resonate.


We believe that at the end of everything, what matters is human. Not technology, not efficiency, just people. 


The premise: the most valuable human connections happen when two people realise they are working on the same question from completely different directions. That moment almost never happens by design on existing platforms. net. is built to make it happen.


We believe in the I-Thou moment — when two people meet as full human beings, with no agenda, nothing to gain, just the pure experience of being heard and understood. We make wandering among humans possible again.


The product centres on one loop:
Post a thought. Someone replies. You accept. You talk.




02 — VOCABULARY
The words we use
These are the only terms used in the product, in the codebase, and in every conversation about net.


Thought  The atomic unit of content. One thought = one AI-generated landscape image + one sentence of text + up to 3 sentences of context on the back.


Post = Invitation  The act of publishing a thought. Posting is itself an invitation — you are opening your thinking to the world. The post is the invite.


Reply  When someone reads a thought and wants to connect, they write a short response. Always written text — not a like, not a reaction, not a preset response.


Accept  When a user receives a reply, they can accept it or ignore it. Accepting opens a conversation. Ignoring removes the notification with no signal sent to the sender.


Conversation  The direct message thread created when a reply is accepted. Lives in Screen 2. Text only. Begins from the reply that started it.


Profile  A user's page: their photo, name, interests, and the full collection of thoughts they have posted.


There is no follow. No connect. No like. No react. If any of these feel necessary during development, come back to the mission and the core loop first.




03 — APP STRUCTURE
Three screens
The entire app is three screens. Nothing more in V1.


Screen 1 — Worlds  The home feed. Users browse thoughts posted by others. Discovery happens here. The notification centre also lives here — it shows when someone has replied to one of your thoughts.


Screen 2 — Conversations  A list of accepted conversation threads, ordered by most recent message. The only place where ongoing connections are visible. 


Screen 3 — Me  The user's own profile. Their photo, name, interests, and every thought they have posted — their full deck.


Bottom navigation: worlds / conversations / me




04 — THE THOUGHT
How a thought works
Every thought has three panels. Swipe left to go deeper. Swipe right to go back. Each panel is darker than the one before — a deliberate signal that you are moving inward, into someone's thinking.


Panel 1 — The front
The face of the thought. What appears in the feed. This is what has to stop someone mid-scroll.


* A landscape image, full width, 4:3. AI-generated from two inputs: the user's posted sentence and their profile photo. The image is never generic — it reflects both the idea and the person who had it.
* The sentence sits at the bottom of the image. One line. Helvetica, white, no box or background behind it.
* Below the sentence: small circular profile photo and name in small uppercase letters.
* Colour: the image is desaturated and low contrast. Quiet. The thought does not perform.
* Right edge: three barely-visible dots, indicating swipe. Not a button — a whisper.


Panel 1 should look like a film still with a caption. If it looks like a social media card, the design is wrong. The bar for visual quality here is high.


Panel 2 — The back
Swipe left once from Panel 1. The background deepens to near-black. You are now inside the thought.


* Up to 600 Characters of context. Where the thought came from. Why it exists right now. The constraint is intentional.


The gap between what the sentence says and what the context reveals is what makes someone want to reply. If the context fully explains the thought, the thought is closed. Keep it open.


Panel 3 — Replies
Swipe left again. The darkest panel. This is where connection begins.


* At the top: a list of replies. Each shows the replier's profile photo, name, and the text of their reply. These have been explicitly accepted — they do not appear here automatically.
* At the bottom: a text input with the label 'reply.' Single line. Placeholder: 'what this thought surfaces in you...'
* The moment the user starts typing, the left-edge bar of the thought turns fully orange. This is the visual signal that their attention has become intention.
* When the reply is sent: a brief haptic pulse and a short visual pulse on the thought. The panels return to Panel 1. The gesture is complete.




05 — COLOUR SYSTEM
Two signals, no decoration
Colour communicates exactly two things in net.: depth (which panel you are on) and warmth (whether a connection is forming). It is never used decoratively.


Depth — panels darken as you swipe left
* Panel 1: landscape image at ~70% brightness, desaturated. Background beneath image: #0C0C0A.
* Panel 2: solid background, #0C0C0A. No image. Warmer undertone than Panel 1.
* Panel 3: darkest, #080604. You are at depth.


Never use pure black (#000000). The slight warmth in the dark values is intentional — it makes the darkness feel like depth, not void.


Left edge bar — warmth
Every thought in the feed has a 2px vertical bar on its left edge. This is the only engagement signal in the product. It has no number attached to it, ever.


* No bar: this thought has had no replies yet.
* Low opacity orange (~30%): at least one reply has been sent to this thought.
* Mid opacity orange (~60%): multiple replies, active engagement.
* Full orange #C4622D: the current user is actively typing a reply right now. The visual equivalent of making eye contact.


Accent — one colour, specific meaning
Single accent: #C4622D. Used only for:


* Left edge bar at full warmth
* Reply input label on Panel 3
* Notification dot on Screen 1
* Post button in creation flow
* Logo mark


If you are considering using orange anywhere else, the answer is no.


Typography
Helvetica Neue throughout. No exceptions.


* Thought sentence (Panel 1): Light 300, 12–13px, white
* Context text (Panel 2): Light 300, 9–10px, muted white
* Names and labels: Regular 400, 7–8px, uppercase, wide letter-spacing
* Reply input: Light 300, 11–12px




06 — SCREEN 1: WORLDS
The feed


Layout
Vertical scroll. Thoughts stack top to bottom. At any scroll position, the user sees approximately 2.5 thoughts — one fully visible, the next partially visible below, a sliver of the one after. The partial visibility creates ambient pull without forcing movement.
Free scroll. No snap-to-thought. The user moves at their own pace.


Notification centre
The notification centre is not a separate screen. It is accessed from Screen 1.


* A small orange dot appears in the top-right corner of the status bar when new replies are waiting.
* Tapping the dot reveals an inline notification list that slides down from the top of the feed. The thoughts below shift down.
* Each notification shows: the replier's name and photo, the first sentence of their reply, and which of your thoughts they replied to.
* Two actions per notification: Accept or Delete.
* Accept: A conversation thread is created in Screen 2. The replier receives special notification — they will simply see a message appear in their conversations.
* Delete: the notification, and the reply is deleted. The replier receives no signal that they were ignored.




07 — SCREEN 2: CONVERSATIONS
Accepted threads only
This screen has one function: show the conversations that are currently happening. It is not a network view. It is not a contact list.


What it shows
* A list of conversation threads ordered by most recent message.
* Each row: profile photo, name, first line of the last message, time since last message.
* Tap to open a thread. Full message view, text only.
* The first message in every thread is the reply that started it — the moment of connection is preserved.


What it does not show
* Not a list of everyone who has replied to your thoughts
* Not a list of people you've viewed or who've viewed you
* Not a network, connection count, or mutual contacts view
* Not a search for people on the platform


Dormancy
A conversation with no messages in 30 or more days is dormant. The row appears visually muted — lower opacity, no urgency markers. It is not archived, deleted, or removed. It is simply quiet.
Do not send push notifications about dormant conversations. Do not surface 'you haven't talked to X in a while.' A relationship going quiet is valid. The product does not judge the pace of human connection.




08 — SCREEN 3: ME
The deck
The profile is not a résumé. It is the collection of thoughts this person has put into the world — their presence on net.




  

What the profile contains - looks like apple contact card 
* Profile photo — blended with the background 
* Name
* Interests — short freeform text, 3 items maximum. Placeholder: 'what you are into right now.' This is not a skills field. It is what is currently alive in this person's thinking.
* Deck — every thought the user has posted, displayed as a scrollable vertical list. Each thought shows Panel 1 only (image + sentence). Tapping opens the full three-panel thought.


What it does not contain
* No follower or connection count
* No job title field
* No 'mutual connections' indicator
* No external links
* No analytics or view counts


Other users can view your profile. You will not know when someone has viewed it. There are no profile view notifications.




09 — CREATION FLOW
How to post a thought
The creation entry point is an understated yet easy button — not a prominent floating action button. Posting should feel like a deliberate choice, not the default action, but also not a burden.


Step 1 — Write the sentence
One large text input. Centered. Placeholder: 'A thought you are in the middle of.' One sentence. The design enforces this through presentation, not a character limit — the input should look like it expects one sentence and nothing more.


Step 2 — Write the context
A smaller textarea below. Placeholder[a]: '600 character of context — where this thought came from.' 
Step 3 — Image generates
As the user types, image generation runs in the background using their sentence and their profile photo as inputs. A 4:3 preview appears above the text fields, the image forming in near real-time. A small animated dot indicates generation is in progress. When complete, the dot disappears. 
Image generation API (Replicate, fal.ai, or similar) using the user's profile photo as a reference image via IP-Adapter or equivalent, with the sentence as the text prompt. The output should feel cinematic and landscape — a world shaped by the person's visual presence and the specific idea they wrote. The same sentence from two different people should produce visibly different images.


Step 4 — Post
A single button at the bottom: 'post.' Tapping it publishes the thought. The user is returned to the feed. The thought appears at the top of their profile deck.
There is no editing after posting. The thought was honest when it was written. If the user wants to remove it, they can delete it from their profile — but deletion does not erase any conversations that began from that thought.




10 — CROSSING
When two thoughts become one
A crossing is a joint thought created collaboratively by two people inside an active conversation. It is the tangible artefact of where their thinking met.


When it becomes available
A crossing is only available after a conversation has accumulated at least 10 exchanges. The option appears right above your texting input screen in the middle, a button (round edged rectangle written crossing) inside the conversation thread — not as a prompt or a notification, but as a small, understated element at the bottom of the thread. It is findable, not pushed.


How it works
Either person can initiate. Initiating does not notify the other person — it opens a draft that sits in the conversation until the other person encounters it.


The crossing has the same structure as a thought:
* A sentence: one line that captures the point where the two thinking threads crossed. Both people write a version. The person who did not initiate chooses which sentence to use, or writes a new one.
* Context: up to 600 characters, written jointly in alternating turns.
* Image: AI-generated from three inputs — the chosen sentence and both people's profile photos. The image reflects the intersection: two visual presences and one shared idea.


Where it lives
The crossing appears on both profiles — in each person's deck, attributed to both. The crossing is also posted to the feed, yet both of you are able to make it both public or private.

00
PHILOSOPHY


The recommendation system is where net.’s philosophy either lives or dies in practice. Every design decision in the product serves one moment: a stranger’s thought stops you mid-scroll, and you feel a pull to respond. The recommendation engine must serve that same moment.
The core premise of net. is that the most valuable human connections happen when two people realize they are working on the same question from completely different directions. This means the system cannot optimize for similarity. Similarity produces recognition, not encounter. The system must optimize for latent connection — the feeling that someone else’s thought, from a completely different surface, is touching the same nerve yours is.
What we do not build
Interest-based matching. Showing a CS student thoughts about coding and a philosophy student thoughts about ethics is expected, and expected does not stop you mid-scroll. People already live inside their interests. They do not need an app to serve those interests back to them.
Pure semantic similarity. Matching sentences that say similar things in similar ways produces echoes, not encounters. Two thoughts that are the same thought in different words generate recognition (“I feel this too”) but not the productive friction that makes someone want to reply.
Social graph proximity. There is no follow, no connect, no mutual contacts in net. The recommendation system must not build a shadow social graph through behavioral signals. Every thought should be able to reach anyone.
What we build instead
A system that surfaces thoughts which are about different things on the surface but are wrestling with the same underlying question. A philosopher writing about when rigor becomes avoidance and an engineer writing about over-optimization are asking the same question. A first-year writing about feeling unmoored in a new city and a fourth-year writing about how the cities were never the point are on the same thread, separated by time. The system finds these threads.
The recommendation system’s job is not to predict what you will find relevant. It is to put the most alive, most unfinished, most gap-filled thinking in front of you and trust that human curiosity does the rest.
01
MINERVA CONTEXT


The initial deployment targets Minerva University students across all active cohorts. This context shapes every technical decision.
Why Minerva first
Minerva solves the hardest cold-start problem in social products: shared context. Every student shares a baseline of common experience — the rotational city model, the seminar-driven pedagogy, the specific intellectual culture. But cohorts are surprisingly siloed. A first-year in Seoul and a fourth-year in Buenos Aires are living parallel versions of a deeply similar experience and almost never talk about it across cohort lines.
This is exactly the “same question, different direction” dynamic net. is built for — except here it is not abstract, it is structural. The system must make a second-year in Hyderabad stop scrolling because a fourth-year in San Francisco posted a thought that hits something they are living through right now.
Meaningful dimensions of difference
Dimension
	Why it matters
	How to use it
	Cohort year
	The most important axis. The same experience lived at different stages produces the richest cross-pollination. A first-year’s thought about feeling lost lands differently when a third-year reads it with two more years behind them.
	Actively boost cross-cohort visibility. Never let the feed become single-cohort dominant.
	Current city
	Valuable inversely to expectation. Students already have proximity-based connection in their current city. The system should surface thoughts from the city you are about to rotate to, or the city someone left two years ago.
	Use as a temporal lens, not a geographic filter. Prioritize city-transition resonance.
	Academic concentration
	The cross-domain bridge. A computational sciences student and an arts & humanities student share seminar culture but see the world through different frameworks. When their thoughts touch the same underlying question, that is where the most interesting replies come from.
	Use to ensure domain diversity in the feed. Never cluster by concentration.
	

At Minerva’s scale — a few hundred students across active cohorts — the hard problem is not ranking. It is getting people to post their first honest thought. The system that gets the first 50 cross-cohort conversations happening matters infinitely more than the system that optimally ranks a feed.
02
THE UNDERLYING QUESTION MODEL


Every thought posted on net. is implicitly asking a question. The sentence “I keep building things nobody asked for” is asking about purpose, validation, whether creative work needs an audience. The sentence “My parents still don’t understand why I left home for this” is also asking about purpose and validation, from a completely different surface.
The matching system operates on this implicit question layer, not on topic labels or interest tags.
How it works
Step 1 — Question extraction. When a thought is posted, an LLM generates a compact representation of the underlying question(s) the thought is wrestling with. This is not keyword extraction. It is: what is this person actually trying to figure out? The output is a short-form question embedding, stored alongside the thought.
Step 2 — Cross-surface matching. The system compares underlying question embeddings across thoughts, looking specifically for pairs where the surface topic differs but the question layer overlaps. High surface similarity with high question similarity is an echo. Low surface similarity with high question similarity is a latent connection. The system optimizes for the second.
Step 3 — Gap detection. The best thoughts to surface are those where the gap between what the sentence says and what the context reveals creates a pull. If the context fully explains the thought, the thought is closed — there is nothing left to reply to. The system should learn to prefer thoughts with open gaps, where the reader’s own experience can fill in what is missing.
Technical implementation
Each thought generates two embedding vectors:
Surface embedding: Standard semantic embedding of the sentence and context text. Used to measure topical similarity.
Question embedding: Generated by prompting an LLM with the thought’s sentence and context, asking: “What underlying question is this person wrestling with? Express it as 1–2 abstract questions that someone from a completely different field might also be asking.” The LLM’s output is then embedded.


The matching score for a given user-thought pair is:


score = question_similarity × (1 + α × surface_distance) × quality_score


Where α is a tunable parameter that rewards surface-level difference (starting at 0.3). The more different the topics but the closer the underlying questions, the higher the match. This is the core mechanism that produces the “seemingly irrelevant but intriguing” experience.
Question taxonomy (emergent, not predefined)
Rather than defining question categories upfront, the system lets them emerge from data. After collecting enough thoughts, cluster the question embeddings to discover what recurring underlying questions the community is actually asking. Early hypotheses for Minerva:
Underlying question
	Surface examples
	What am I becoming vs. what I planned to become?
	Thoughts about changing majors, outgrowing friendships, feeling different after a city rotation, career doubt
	When does structure help and when does it constrain?
	Thoughts about seminar formats, self-discipline, creative process, relationships with routine
	What do I owe the place I came from?
	Thoughts about home, cultural identity, guilt about privilege, distance from family
	How do I know if what I am doing matters?
	Thoughts about impact, side projects, academic work feeling abstract, wanting to build something real
	What does it mean to understand someone different from me?
	Thoughts about cross-cultural friction, language barriers, intellectual disagreement, empathy limits
	

These are hypotheses. The real taxonomy will emerge from what Minerva students actually post. The system should be built to discover categories, not enforce them.
03
REPLY-BASED LEARNING


The recommendation system’s ground truth is not views, not dwell time, not clicks. It is: did this thought produce a real conversation? The funnel from impression to conversation is the learning signal.
The engagement funnel
Signal
	What it measures
	Weight
	Panel 1 → Panel 2 (swipe to context)
	Curiosity. The sentence created a gap worth exploring.
	Low — directional only
	Panel 2 → Panel 3 (swipe to replies)
	Intent. The context deepened interest enough to consider replying.
	Medium
	Typing begins in Panel 3
	Commitment. The user found words for what the thought surfaced.
	Medium-high
	Reply sent
	Expression. A written thought was offered to a stranger.
	High
	Reply accepted
	Mutual recognition. The thought’s author saw value in the response.
	Very high
	Conversation reaches 10+ exchanges
	Sustained connection. The initial spark became something real.
	Highest — this is ground truth
	

What the system learns from replies
Cross-domain generativity. When a humanities student replies to an engineering student’s thought, and that reply gets accepted and the conversation sustains — the system learns that this specific cross-domain pairing is generative. Over time, a map of productive distances emerges. Philosophy × engineering might be highly generative. Philosophy × philosophy might not be, because there is no gap to cross.
Temporal resonance patterns. If thoughts about city transitions consistently draw replies from students two cohorts ahead, the system learns that temporal distance on shared experience is a reliable connection pattern. It can then boost similar pairings.
Question cluster validation. When two thoughts from different surface domains lead to sustained conversation, the underlying question embeddings of both thoughts are validated as genuinely connected. This reinforces the question taxonomy with real behavioral data.
Quality signal. Thoughts that generate multiple accepted replies across different domains are high-quality thoughts — they have open gaps that many different people can enter. The system should give these thoughts longer feed life.
04
CATEGORIZATION SYSTEM


The previous spec proposed 8 Level-1 domains with 80 Level-2 subcategories. That system is designed for a scaled product with millions of users. For Minerva v1, it introduces cognitive load during onboarding, assumes users know what they want to encounter (they don’t), and pushes people into interest silos that work against the product’s mission.
The v1 categorization is minimal, emergent, and invisible to users.
What users see: nothing
There are no topic tags on thoughts. No category filters in the feed. No interest taxonomy during onboarding. The user posts a thought, and the system figures out where it lives. The user scrolls the feed, and the system figures out what to show them. The only user-facing input is the three-item interests field on the profile, which captures what someone is into right now — not a structured selection from a predefined list.
What the system sees: three layers
Layer
	Source
	Purpose
	Profile signal
	Name, interests field (freeform), academic concentration, cohort year, current city
	Lightweight identity context. Used for diversity constraints, not primary matching.
	Thought signal
	Surface embedding + question embedding for each posted thought
	Primary matching input. What this person is actually thinking about, in their own words.
	Behavioral signal
	Swipe depth, reply patterns, conversation sustain rate, cross-domain engagement
	Learning input. What kinds of encounters actually produce connection for this person.
	

Emergent categories
After accumulating enough thoughts and reply data, the system clusters question embeddings to discover the community’s actual question landscape. These clusters are internal only — never exposed to users as labels or filters. They serve two purposes:
Feed diversity. Ensure the feed does not over-index on any single question cluster. If 60% of recent thoughts are about identity transitions, the system should still surface thoughts from less common clusters to maintain the feeling of wandering through a world of thinking.
Cross-cluster bridging. Identify which question clusters have the highest reply-to-conversation conversion rates when paired together. These productive pairings become the system’s primary recommendation signal.
05
FEED RANKING LOGIC


Phase 1: Pre-data (first 2–4 weeks)
Before the system has enough behavioral data to learn from, the feed uses a simple rule-based approach:
Recency. Newer thoughts rank higher, with a gentle decay curve (not strict chronological).
Cross-cohort diversity. No single cohort may exceed 40% of visible thoughts at any scroll position. This is a hard constraint, not a soft boost.
Concentration diversity. Adjacent thoughts in the feed should come from different academic concentrations when possible.
Quality floor. Thoughts with extremely short context or generic sentences are deprioritized. The bar is: does this sound like a real person in the middle of something real?


Show everyone everything. Let the content be the filter. At Minerva’s scale, every thought should be seen by most people. The ranking determines order, and order should prioritize cross-cohort resonance.


Phase 2: Learning (after sufficient reply data)
Once the system has observed enough reply → accept → conversation patterns, the ranking becomes:


rank = Q × w₁ + D × w₂ + F × w₃ + R × w₄


Component
	Description
	Initial weight
	Q — Question resonance
	Underlying question similarity between this thought and the viewer’s own posted thoughts, weighted by surface distance. The core matching signal.
	40%
	D — Diversity score
	How different this thought is from what the viewer has recently seen. Measures cohort distance, concentration distance, and question cluster distance.
	25%
	F — Freshness
	Recency with gentle decay. Ensures the feed does not stagnate.
	20%
	R — Reply quality
	How often this thought’s previous replies have converted to sustained conversations. A proxy for how open and alive the thought is.
	15%
	

What is explicitly excluded from ranking
View counts. The number of people who saw a thought has no bearing on whether it should be shown to the next person.
Reply counts. A thought with many replies is not necessarily better. The warmth bar handles engagement visibility. The ranking system does not double-count it.
Social proximity. There is no friend graph, no mutual connection signal, no “people like you also liked” logic. Every thought can reach anyone.
Profile completeness. A sparse profile does not penalize a thought’s ranking. The thought speaks for itself.
06
DISCOVERY CONTROL


The previous spec proposed a 5-notch slider (Tuned ↔ Serendipity) and 4 lens filters (Personal / Study / Career / Culture). Both are removed for v1.
Why no slider
A discovery slider asks users to configure a ranking parameter. Nobody wakes up thinking “I want 35% novelty weighting today.” The cognitive load is misplaced. The feed should learn how a person wants to encounter thinking by watching what they actually do — not by asking them to set a dial.
Why no lens filters
Four categorical lenses encourage a consumption mindset: “I want career thoughts right now.” But net. is about encounter, not consumption. The product spec says the feed shows approximately 2.5 thoughts with ambient pull. That is a browsing-and-being-caught experience, not a filtered-search experience. Categorical filters cut against this by letting people pre-decide what they are open to before they have seen it.
What replaces them
Adaptive learning. The system watches swipe depth, reply patterns, and conversation outcomes to understand what kinds of encounters work for each person. If someone consistently engages with cross-domain thoughts, the feed leans into more surface-level difference. If someone consistently engages with thoughts from adjacent concentrations, the feed respects that pattern. No configuration required.
For Minerva v1, a single optional toggle may be offered:


Control
	Default
	Behavior
	"My cohort / All cohorts"
	All cohorts (always)
	When set to My cohort, the feed shows only thoughts from the user’s cohort year. When set to All cohorts, the standard cross-cohort diversity rules apply. Default is always All cohorts.
	

Even this toggle should be evaluated for removal if data shows it reduces cross-cohort connection. The entire purpose of the Minerva deployment is cross-cohort discovery. A filter that disables it should exist only if there is a clear use case.
07
ONBOARDING


The previous spec proposed a two-step onboarding: select 2–4 Level-1 domains, then select 6–12 Level-2 chips. This is replaced with a flow that captures identity context without imposing interest categories.
Step 1 — Identity
Name, profile photo, cohort year, current city. These are structural facts, not preferences. They take 30 seconds.
Step 2 — Interests (freeform)
Three freeform text fields. Placeholder: “What you are into right now.” No predefined list. No chips. No categories. The user writes what is alive in their thinking in their own words. These become part of the profile signal and are embedded for lightweight matching, but they are not the primary recommendation input.
Step 3 — First thought
The onboarding ends by asking the user to post their first thought. This is the most important onboarding step, because the system cannot recommend anything to someone who has not revealed what they are thinking about. The first thought becomes the user’s primary signal in the recommendation system.
The onboarding is complete when the user has posted one thought. Everything else is optional context. The system learns from what people write, not from what categories they select.

	interests are freeform strings, not references to a taxonomy

	Learned. The productive distance map.
	

What is not stored
No interest taxonomy tables. No L1/L2 category assignments. No user-to-category mappings. No lens score per thought. No social graph edges. The system’s intelligence lives in embeddings and behavioral patterns, not in predefined categories.
09
INTERNAL METRICS


These metrics are for the team only. None are ever exposed to users.
Primary (the only ones that matter)
Metric
	Definition
	Target
	Cross-cohort conversation rate
	% of accepted replies that come from a different cohort than the thought author
	> 60%
	Reply-to-conversation conversion
	% of sent replies that get accepted
	25–40% (too high means no friction; too low means bad matching)
	Conversation sustain rate
	% of conversations that reach 10+ exchanges
	> 30%
	First-thought completion
	% of new users who post a thought within 24 hours of signup
	> 70%
	

Secondary (diagnostic, not targets)
Swipe-through rate (P1 → P2 → P3) — measures thought quality and curiosity generation. Average reply length — measures depth of engagement. Cohort distribution in feed — measures diversity constraint effectiveness. Time to first reply on a new thought — measures feed circulation. Question cluster distribution — measures whether the community’s thinking is diverse or converging.
If you find yourself wanting to add a metric, ask: does this measure whether two strangers had a real conversation? If not, it is probably noise.
10
NON-NEGOTIABLES


No interest taxonomy exposed to users. Users never see categories, tags, or labels on thoughts. They never select topics from a list. The system’s categorization is entirely internal and emergent.
Cross-cohort diversity is a hard constraint. The feed must always contain meaningful representation from multiple cohorts. This is not a soft boost that gets overridden by relevance scoring. It is a structural guarantee.
Underlying question matching is the primary signal. Surface topic similarity is never the dominant ranking factor. If two thoughts are about the same topic from the same perspective, that is an echo, not a recommendation. The system must prefer productive distance over comfortable proximity.
Reply behavior is ground truth. No amount of implicit signal (views, dwell time, scrolling patterns) overrides the explicit signal of a written reply being accepted and leading to sustained conversation. The system learns from what people do with intention.
The system never prompts engagement. No “you might want to reply to this” notifications. No “this thought is trending” labels. No algorithmic urgency. The feed presents thinking. The user decides what to engage with. The system observes and learns.
Quality of creation matters more than quality of ranking. If the creation flow consistently produces honest, specific, unfinished thoughts — the kind with a gap that invites someone in — then almost any ordering of the feed will work. If people post polished, closed statements, no algorithm will save the experience. Invest in the prompt, not the ranking.

01  Design Principles
net. has one visual job: make a thought feel worth stopping for. Every decision — layout, colour, type, spacing — serves that single moment.
The feed is warm, quiet, analogue in feeling. It does not perform energy. Cards sit on warm off-white. Images are desaturated and cinematic. Type is minimal and restrained. The product recedes so the thought can come forward.


The design recedes. The thought comes forward.

05  Feed Layout
The feed is a vertical stack of thought cards on warm off-white (#F5F0EA). At any scroll position the user sees approximately 2.5 cards — one fully in view, the next partially visible, a sliver of the third. Partial visibility creates ambient pull without forcing movement.
Free scroll. No snap-to-card. The user moves at their own pace.


The card should look like a film still with a caption. If it reads as a social media post, the design is wrong.

Every card has three panels. Swipe left to go deeper. Swipe right to go back. Horizontal slide — no fade, no bounce. Each panel occupies the full screen. Each is darker than the one before.

Not a separate screen. Slides down inline from the top of Screen 1 when the orange circle is tapped. The feed below shifts down to accommodate it.

09  Crossing & Shift
Two collaborative artefacts available after 10 or more exchanges. Both are quiet — findable, never prompted. Both require both people to complete.


CROSSING — WHERE TWO THOUGHTS MET
* Joint thought: sentence + up to 3 sentences context + AI image from both profile photos.
* Both write a sentence version. Non-initiator chooses or writes new.
* Context written jointly in alternating turns, up to 3 sentences total.
* Lives only on both profiles. Never in the feed.
SHIFT — WHERE THINKING MOVED
* Two-halved card: Before → After for each person, side by side.
* Each half: profile photo + before sentence (muted) + after sentence (full).
* No AI image. The two arcs are the content.
* Appears in both people’s Worlds feed. The conversation always stays private.


The crossing is where two thoughts met. The shift is proof that something moved.

11  Non-Negotiables
These decisions must survive the build. If a technical constraint creates pressure to change one of them, flag it before changing it.


Replies require acceptance before they appear
A reply waits in the notification centre until accepted. This is the gate that keeps the product from becoming a comment platform. Do not remove it, even under time pressure.
No metrics exposed to users
No counts of any kind. The left-edge warmth bar is the only engagement signal, and it is visual, not numerical.
No editing after posting
A thought is final when posted. Editing would retroactively change what someone responded to.
Replies must be written text
No preset replies, no emojis as primary action, no like button. If a user cannot find words, they do not reply. That friction is correct.
Dormancy is valid
A conversation going quiet is not a failure state. No automated nudges, no reconnect suggestions.
The interests field is not a skills field
It asks what the user is into right now. Not professional skills. Not a job title. Never use the word “skills” near it.
The crossing is private
Never in the feed, never in discovery, never visible to anyone outside the conversation it came from.
The shift conversation stays private
The shift appears in the feed. The thread never does. Only the arc — before and after — is ever visible.
Neither crossing nor shift is ever prompted
The options exist quietly after 10 exchanges. People find them when ready, or they don’t.
